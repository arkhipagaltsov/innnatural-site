CRISPR: The Word Processor for DNA

How scientists are editing genes as easily as fixing a typo in a document.

What if we were able to correct a mutation in our DNA as easily as correcting a misspelled word in a text document? This has been a dream of scientists in biology for decades. Today, a revolutionary tool called CRISPR enables scientists to do just that, which has the potential to drastically alter the course of medicine, agriculture, and our understanding of biology in the future.

Borrowed from nature

CRISPR did not begin as a man-made invention in a laboratory. It was discovered in bacteria. For millions of years, bacteria have been using a primitive form of CRISPR to protect themselves against viruses. They use a "mugshot" of a virus's DNA to identify it. If a bacterium encounters a virus it has encountered before, it uses microscopic scissors to cut up the virus's DNA into pieces.

Scientists discovered that if bacteria can use these scissors to locate a specific virus's DNA, humans can use it to locate any DNA.

How it works

To change a gene, scientists use a simplified two-component system:

GPS (Guide RNA): A specially designed molecule that is designed to locate the precise genetic typo.
Scissors (Cas9): An enzyme that follows GPS to the typo and makes a precise cut in the DNA sequence.

Once this is done, the body’s natural repair mechanisms will begin. It is in this process that scientists can now instruct the body to knock out a problematic gene or replace it with a corrected and healthy version of itself.

The real-world impact

Genetic engineering used to be like trying to edit a book by spilling some ink on a page and hoping it spelled a word. It is now like using a scalpel instead of an ink spill.

This is now yielding astonishing results:

Curing genetic diseases: Genetic diseases such as sickle-cell anemia are caused by a typo in a person’s DNA sequence. Today, scientists are using this tool in clinics to correct this very typo and cure sickle-cell anemia.

Resilient agriculture: Scientists are engineering crops that can withstand droughts and resist pests, skipping the generations-long process of traditional cross-breeding techniques.

Targeting cancer: We are actively working on using the human immune system more effectively in fighting cancer by reprogramming the patient's immune cells.

The Ethical Frontier

With such power comes unprecedented responsibility. As this technology becomes more and more accessible, we are faced with gigantic questions of ethics.

Firstly, is it curing a debilitating illness, or is it "designer" enhancement of characteristics such as height and intelligence? Secondly, is it responsible to meddle with the DNA of a species in the wild, for instance, by engineering mosquitoes to prevent malaria, and risk causing unforeseen consequences in an entire ecosystem? Thirdly, as this technology becomes available in the market and "miraculous cures" become more and more available, will they only be available to those who can afford them?

What's next for you?

The time of merely reading the instruction manual for life is behind us—the time for writing our own version of it has arrived. Due to the rapid development of CRISPR technology, the rules for its application are being written now.

You don't have to be a geneticist to have a say in the matter. Be aware of the new biotech regulations coming out in your country and begin asking your healthcare providers about gene-based medicine. The age of CRISPR technology is not science fiction—it is now, and we are writing the rules for it.
